Chris Martins romance with girlfriend Dakota Johnson seems to have inspired lyrics for new Coldpl

June 20, 2024
blog

THEY are the masters of reinvention, having morphed from acoustic pop rock to experimental electronic sounds and everything in between.

Now Coldplay have done it again with their hotly anticipated comeback album.

One fan guaranteed to be pleased with the output is frontman Chris Martin’s actress girlfriend Dakota Johnson, who appears to have inspired some of the gushing lyrics.

I was given an early listen today to the first tracks from the Brit stars’ eighth studio album Everyday Life — which sees them mix anthemic pop choruses and funk-inspired basslines on double A-side singles Orphans and Arabesque.

But what fans really want to hear is what’s on Chris’s mind in the lyrics after four turbulent years that have seen him date actresses Jennifer Lawrence, Anabelle Wallis and, most recently, Fifty Shades of Grey star Dakota. And as Chris revealed tonight, he has opened up.

Speaking for the first time about the album, the singer said: “Some of it is very ­personal, about real things in my life, and some is about things I see or we see, and some of it’s about trying to empathise with what ­others are going through.”

In Arabesque, in lyrics that appear to be about his romance with Dakota, he sings: “I could be you, you could be me. Two raindrops in the same sea.

“You could be me, I could be you. Two angles of the same view.”

Tellingly, the second verse is in French — in which Dakota is fluent — hinting the track is a love letter to his girlfriend after they reignited their romance following a rough patch early this year. It translates loosely as: “Like two streams, who gather, to make big rivers.”

And as Chris added in an interview with Radio 1’s Annie Mac, he no longer worries what people think about what he says.

He explained: “There was something about our last tour that made me at peace with speaking openly and not minding if people disagree. You can just speak ­completely freely and let all the colours of life come through.”

Second track Orphans sees Chris tackling the Syrian crisis in moving lyrics about a young woman living in the heart of a war zone.

It’s a moving and emotive subject but set to an upbeat backdrop that includes black observations where he imagines himself in the chaos.

He explains: “One of the things that might help people have a better time is to put themselves in other people’s shoes, whether that’s these kids who have to leave Syria, or who grew up in Baltimore, or whatever it might be. Rather than judging from afar, maybe to think: ‘I wonder what it’s like to be there’.”

In a quirky move, the band unveiled the track listing today in classified adverts in local newspapers near to where they each grew up.

Sitting alongside notices for second-hand fridge freezers and car boot sales, their ads confirm the record is split into two halves titled Sunrise and Sunset.

Despite this, there’s nothing bargain basement about this sophisticated and insightful new release.
